Information Retrieval and Search Engines
========================================

Information retrieval is the process of obtaining relevant information from a large collection of unstructured documents.
The goal of an information retrieval system is to find material that satisfies an information need from within large collections.
Search engines like Google, Bing, and DuckDuckGo are large-scale information retrieval systems used by billions of people daily.

The Vector Space Model (VSM) represents both documents and queries as vectors in a high-dimensional term space.
In VSM, each unique term in the corpus corresponds to one dimension of the vector space.
Documents that are more similar to the query will have vectors that point in a similar direction in the vector space.

TF (Term Frequency) measures how often a specific term appears in a document relative to the total number of words in that document.
A high TF value means the term appears frequently in the document and is likely important to its content.
IDF (Inverse Document Frequency) measures how rare or common a term is across all documents in the entire corpus.
Terms that appear in every document have a low IDF because they carry little discriminating information.
TF-IDF is the product of TF and IDF and is one of the most important weighting schemes in information retrieval.
A high TF-IDF score means the term is frequent in that specific document but rare across the whole collection.

Cosine similarity measures the cosine of the angle between two vectors in the vector space.
A cosine similarity of 1.0 means the two vectors point in exactly the same direction and are perfectly similar.
A cosine similarity of 0.0 means the two vectors are orthogonal and share no common terms at all.
Cosine similarity is preferred over Euclidean distance because it is independent of document length.

Apache Lucene is a high-performance, full-featured text search engine library originally written in Java by Doug Cutting.
Lucene implements the Vector Space Model and BM25 algorithm for ranking documents by relevance to a query.
ZendSearch Lucene is a PHP port of Apache Lucene that allows PHP developers to build full-text search applications.
Lucene uses an inverted index which maps each unique term to the list of documents that contain it.

Indexing is the process of analyzing documents, extracting terms, and storing them in the index for fast retrieval.
Stemming is a technique that reduces words to their root form so that run, running, and ran all match the same stem.
Stop words are very common words like the, is, at, and on that are typically removed during indexing to save space.
Tokenization is the process of splitting text into individual terms or tokens during the indexing and querying process.

Precision measures the fraction of retrieved documents that are actually relevant to the search query.
Recall measures the fraction of all relevant documents in the collection that were successfully retrieved.
Precision at k, written as Precision@k, measures how many of the top k returned results are relevant.
Mean Average Precision, known as MAP, evaluates ranking quality by averaging precision scores across multiple queries.
The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall and provides a single balanced evaluation metric.

Query expansion improves retrieval performance by adding synonyms or related terms to the original query.
Relevance feedback allows users to mark results as relevant or irrelevant to refine the search results automatically.
Elasticsearch and Apache Solr are modern production-grade search engines built on top of the Lucene library.
